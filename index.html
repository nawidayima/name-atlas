The finalists

Three names that survived everything

Finalist · 01

Kavi कवि · KAH-vee

The seer-scientist

In the Ṛgveda, a kavi is not merely a poet. It is the visionary sage who perceives ṛta — the hidden order of the cosmos — and puts it into words. It is the closest thing Sanskrit has to a single word for "one who looks at the world and sees what is really there," which is a fair job description for a scientist and a poet alike.

Phonetically it is a sibling of Kai, Levi, and Ravi — it sounds native in a 2027 American classroom while being nobody else's name. And it works instantly, unexplained, in both India and the West.

Finalist · 02

Miro MEE-roh

Loving-kindness, well-traveled

The East-Asia transmission name we hoped existed — and it is genuine at every link. Sanskrit mitra (friend) became maitrī (loving-kindness), became Maitreya, the future Buddha — who crossed China into Korea as Mireuk and Japan as Miroku. Korea's most beloved sculpture, the seventh-century Gilt-Bronze Pensive Bodhisattva, is Mireuk; Silla's hwarang warriors believed themselves his incarnations.

Bonus verified lives: Miro is independently a common Finnish name, a Slavic name from mir — "peace, world" — and Joan Miró supplies the art-house cool. One name, three true origins, three continents.

Finalist · 03

Aruni अरुणि · ah-ROO-nee

The first empiricist, at dawn

The deepest science narrative we found anywhere. Uddālaka Āruṇi, Chāndogya Upaniṣad, ~8th century BCE, teaches his son by demonstration: dissolve salt in water overnight — you cannot see it, but taste anywhere and it is there. Scholars genuinely rank him among history's first natural philosophers; popular essays call him "the first scientist."

The name itself comes from aruṇa — dawn-red — the crimson charioteer who pulls the sun into the sky. Inquiry, dawn, and cosmos folded into one word. Zero recorded US births, ever: nobody on Earth shares it, yet it sounds like a neighbor of Aria and Luna.

The wildcards

Bolder, quieter, riskier

Zen 禅 · ध्यान

The famous journey itself

The 1,500-year chain in the header — dhyāna → jhāna → chán → sŏn → zen — worn as a name. One syllable, unmissable meaning, gender-flexible.

277 US births/yr · at the top-1000 doorstep · reads a little on-the-nose

Meru सुमेरु · 수미산

The mountain the stars turn around

The axial peak of Buddhist cosmology — Sumeru, "wondrous Meru" — carried into Japanese as Shumisen, Korean as Sumisan. The still center of a turning universe.

0 recorded US births · boldest pick · expect "did you invent it?"

Reva रेवा · RAY-vah

The leaping river

The old Sanskrit name of the Narmadā — "she who leaps." A real river name with a modern sound: Nova- and Ava-adjacent, quiet, stable.

63 US births/yr · flat for a decade · zero spike risk · F-leaning

The middle-name shelf

Names too heavy, too storied, or too ours for first-name duty — perfect one slot back, where a name can be all meaning and no logistics.

    The boy who waited at Death's door and traded every riches for one true answer. The family motto, hidden in the middle.

  • Tara

    Star, and "she who carries across" — the guide over the ocean of saṃsāra.

  • Saryu

    Your own river. The lineage says its name quietly, and the child carries it everywhere.
