The finalists

Three names that survived everything

Finalist · 01

Kavi कवि · KAH-vee

The seer-scientist

In the Ṛgveda, a kavi is a seer before he is a poet: the sage who perceives ṛta, the hidden order of the cosmos, and puts it into words. Sanskrit comes closest here to a single word for looking at the world and seeing what is actually there. That describes a scientist as well as it describes a poet.

Phonetically it sits beside Kai, Levi, and Ravi, so it sounds native in a 2027 American classroom while being nobody else's name. It needs no explanation in India or in the West.

Finalist · 02

Miro MEE-roh

Loving-kindness, well-traveled

The East Asia transmission name we hoped existed, genuine at every link. Sanskrit mitra (friend) became maitrī (loving-kindness), then Maitreya, the future Buddha, who crossed China into Korea as Mireuk and Japan as Miroku. Korea's most famous sculpture, the seventh-century Gilt-bronze Pensive Bodhisattva, is Mireuk. Silla's hwarang warriors believed themselves his incarnations.

Miro also has two unrelated lives of its own: a common Finnish given name, and a Slavic short form from mir, "peace, world." Joan Miró adds the art association for free.

Finalist · 03

Aruni अरुणि · ah-ROO-nee

The first empiricist, at dawn

The oldest science story on this page. Uddālaka Āruṇi, in the Chāndogya Upaniṣad (around the 8th century BCE), teaches his son by demonstration. Salt dissolved overnight in water: invisible, yet taste anywhere and it is there. The Indologist Walter Ruben ranked him among the earliest natural philosophers on record.

The name comes from aruṇa, dawn-red: Aruṇa is the crimson charioteer who pulls the sun into the sky. No US birth record has ever listed it, yet it sounds like a neighbor of Aria and Luna.

Verdict: the origin story. If the science story matters most to us, nobody else will ever have this name.

The wildcards

Bolder, quieter, riskier

Zen 禅 · ध्यान

The famous journey itself

The 1,500-year chain from the top of this page, worn as a name: dhyāna, jhāna, chán, sŏn, zen. One syllable, meaning intact, gender-flexible.

277 US births/yr · at the top-1000 doorstep · reads a little on-the-nose

Meru सुमेरु · 수미산

The mountain the stars turn around

The axial peak of Buddhist cosmology, Sumeru or "wondrous Meru," carried into Japanese as Shumisen and Korean as Sumisan. The still center of a turning universe.

0 recorded US births · boldest pick · expect "did you invent it?"

Reva रेवा · RAY-vah

The leaping river

An old Sanskrit name of the Narmadā, "she who leaps." A real river name with a modern sound, next door to Nova and Ava.

63 US births/yr · flat for a decade · zero spike risk · F-leaning

The middle-name shelf

Names too heavy or too storied for first-name duty. One slot back, a name can be all meaning and no logistics.

  • Nachiketa

    The boy who waited at Death's door and refused every treasure for one true answer. A family motto, hidden in the middle.

  • Tara

    Star, and "she who carries across": the guide over the ocean of saṃsāra.

  • Saryu

    A river from the family's old map, flowing past Ayodhya. It says where we began without announcing it.

One last proof that words travel: Canon cameras are named for Kannon, the Japanese form of Avalokiteśvara. Compassion itself, arrived in Japan and then in your pocket. A good name goes far.
